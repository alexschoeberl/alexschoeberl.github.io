UHD, 4K, 8K. Where are we going?

Opinion · Feb 10, 2015

Over the last few years, we saw a strong growth in smartphone screen resolutions. The first iPhone from 2007 was able to display 480×320 pixels. When the HTC One (M8) was presented in 2014, some people were disappointed by the resolution being only 1920×1080 pixels. According to these figures, the number of pixels increased by a factor of 14 in only 7 years. The surface area hardly doubled in the same time. By now smartphones with even higher resolutions are available. But does this competition among the manufactures for the smallest pixels even make sense?

The surface area isn't the only factor to determine the ideal resolution. This becomes apparent when I'm comparing my 50-inch TV to my 5-inch smartphone. Both of them display 1920×1080 pixels. However, since my distance from the TV is about ten times greater, it's resolution is acceptable. In general, the resolution of a display is quoted as the number of horizontal pixels by the number of vertical pixels. Full HD refers to a resolution of 1920×1080 pixels, while UHD (Ultra High Definition) refers to 3840×2160 pixels. 2K and 4K indicate resolutions of approximately 2000 and 4000 horizontal pixels, respectively. Looking at the number of pixels and the screen size, you can calculate the ppi, or pixels per inch. This figure is a measurement of the pixel density, also called resolution. The HTC One comes with a pixel density of 441 ppi, the Oppo Find 7 with 538 ppi.

You can already buy computer monitors with an UHD resolution at affordable prices. Prices for high resolution TV panels will decrease subsequently. Even 4k tablets targeting the consumer market are already in the works. Two factors determine the ideal resolution: surface area and working distance from the user. The subjective size of a smartphone can be as large as a movie screen if held directly in front of one's face. Therefore, and in this special case, the respective resolution should be identical. In order to achieve this, the indiviual pixels on the smartphone have to be much smaller. The virtual reality headset Oculus Rift places it's OLED displays right in front of the user's eyes. As their resolution resembles those of modern smartphones, you can still make out individual pixels.

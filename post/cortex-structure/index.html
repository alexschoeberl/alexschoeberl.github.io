Cellular Structure of the Neocortex

Article · Feb 12, 2017

The neocortex is the phylogenetically newest and most developed part of the cerebrum. It is its outermost structure and spans across most of the telencephalon. The neocortex is responsible for processing sensory information and controlling voluntary movement. It receives neural signals and interprets them, giving them a meaning. In comparison to other mammals, apart from dolphins, hominids and especially humans have exceptionally large neocortices. They play the key role in all the characteristic cognitive abilities of humans. These abilities include consciousness, memory, language and perception.

As the human cortex consists of billions of neurons, not all of its larger scale structure is known in detail. Understanding the cytoarchitecture will give leads on the cortical functions. The neocortex has many connections to and is in control of many parts of the body. It is vital to enabling our way of living.

Anatomy

In mammals, the brain and the spinal cord make up the central nervous system. The brain consists of the cerebrum, the cerebellum and the brain stem. The cerebrum is separated into two distinct cerebral hemispheres by the sagittal fissure. The term telencephalon refers to the same part of the brain. It surrounds the central diencephalon, which contains the third ventricle and can be seen as part of the brain stem. The cerebrum is the largest part of the human brain. It expands around the fluid-filled lateral ventricles and contains the cerebral cortex and other structures, including the basal ganglia and the olfactory bulb.

The cerebral cortex is the outer layer of the telencephalon. It consists of gray matter and encapsulates the underlying white matter. The cortex is enveloped by protective membranes called meninges. It can be further divided into the allocortex and the notably larger isocortex. These two parts are distinguishable by their cell structure. The phylogenetically newer isocortex is also called neocortex. The neocortex is 2-4 millimeters thick and found only in mammals. Areas where the isocortex changes into the allocortex are called proisocortex.

Cortical Areas

The human cerebral cortex is divided into two cortices by the sagittal fissure. A flat fibre bundle called corpus callosum connects these two cortices. The cortical hemispheres can be subdivided into the frontal, parietal, occipital and temporal lobe. Hidden between the temporal and the frontal lobe lies the so-called insula. The lateral fissure is the spacing between those lobes. The superior lateral gyrus can be found neighbouring this fissure. The central sulcus separates the frontal from the parietal lobe. The precentral and the postcentral gyrus are flanking this sulcus.

Beside the lobes, gyri, sulci and fissures, the human cerebral cortex comprises different areas. These areas can be distinguished from one another by their microscopic structure and function. Korbinian Brodmann divided the cortex into more than 50 of them. There are three types of areas. Sensory areas analyse sensory information. Part of these are the visual areas, which are in the occipital lobe. The auditory areas can be found in the temporal lobe. The somatic sensory areas, which are responsible for bodily perception, are part of the parietal lobe.

Another type of areas are the motor control areas. They are responsible for voluntary movement. The most important of these areas are found in the frontal lobe. The primary motor cortex lies in the precentral gyrus.

The largest areas belong to the third type. They are called association areas. Each sensory cortex is accompanied by an association area. Even the primary motor cortex is assisted by association areas. Higher order association areas are responsible for many of the characteristic cognitive abilities of humans, including language function. Important association areas can be found in the prefrontal, posterior parietal, and inferotemporal cortex.

Layers

Although there are quite some differences in the microscopic structure of distinct areas of the neocortex, a general model can be defined. The neocortex consists of six stacked but tightly interconnected layers, each with a typical distribution of cell types and neural connections. These layers lie parallel to the surface and are numbered with Roman numerals starting from the outermost layer.

  • The supragranular layers (I-III) are responsible for intracortical connections and communication. These connections can be to and from other areas of the same or opposite hemisphere.
    • Layer I, the molecular layer, contains only few neuronal cell bodies. It consists mostly of axons, dendrites and glia cells. Pyramidal neurons of different sizes can be found in the underlying layers. These cells often emit large dendritic trees into higher layers.
    • Layer II, the external granular layer, can be observed as a prominent structure during foetal life and infancy. It consists of small pyramidal and stellate neurons. In the developed human brain, the external granular layer fuses with the underlying pyramidal layer.
    • Layer III, the external pyramidal layer, contains many pyramidal cells and other neurons with vertically oriented intracortical axons.
  • Layer IV, the internal granular layer, receives many connections from the thalamus, but also from the other hemisphere. It contains pyramidal and stellate neurons. Their short axons usually terminate in other local layers.
  • The infragranular layers (V-VI) primarily handle communication with subcortical regions like the basal ganglia and the brain stem.
    • Layer V, the internal pyramidal layer, contains large pyramidal cells. In the motor cortex, some of these cells are particularly large and send their axons directly down the spinal cord, through the corticospinal tract. Their dendrites reach into every layer, but most of them can be found in Layers V and VI.
    • Layer VI, the multiform layer, contains neurons of different forms. Many of these cells send their axons to the thalamus.

Pyramidal neurons are an important type of cells found in the neocortex. Their cell body has a triangular shape. They emit a single large dendrite with several branches from the pyramid's apex. Shorter basal dendritic trees emerge from the pyramid's base. Most of the neurons in the cortex are of this type. Stellate neurons are the other important type of cells. As the name suggests, these cells are star-like shaped. They have clearly shorter neurites. Stellate neurons can be classified as interneurons and are primarily inhibitory. Other neuronal cell types of the cortex include basket cells and chandelier cells.

Neurons transmitting sensory information from the eyes, ears and skin all provide synapses in the thalamus. There, the signal is passed on to thalamic neurons, whose axons pass through the internal capsule to different areas of the neocortex. These axons terminate in the internal granular layer, which is therefore most developed in primary sensory areas. The primary sensory cortex is therefore also called granular cortex. This layer can be exceptionally small or even missing in primary motor areas.

The infragranular layers are most prominent in primary motor areas. Giant pyramidal cells send their axons down the spinal cord, to control voluntary movement. Some of their dendrites reach all the way up to Layer I. This type of cortex is called agranular cortex.

The supragranular layers are particularly high developed in humans. It manages communication between one cortical region and others. Association fibres are likely to arise from layer III and end in layers I or II. They can connect different lobes or even hemispheres.

Columns

The neocortex can be vertically categorized into different histological and functional areas. Additionally, it is believed that a single area can be further divided into small, relatively independent vertical structures. These structures are called cortical columns. There is a clear predominance of vertical connections between neurons in the cortical layers. A column spans all layers of the cortex. The concept behind this architecture is that every columns is a functional unit and responsible for a single, independent task. The columns of an area work in parallel on a bigger problem. However, there exist different, inconsistent definitions of cortical columns. Cortical columns are especially well-documented for the sensory areas. There, vertical microcircuits have been observed, which are activated by the same receptive field, while surrounding circuits stayed mostly inactive. It is implied that these columns are isolated to some degree. Basket cells play a key role to provide this form of isolation.

Sometimes two types of cortical columns are defined, hypercolumns and microcolumns. During foetal development, neurons are born from stem cells close to the cerebral ventricles. They move along radial glia cells to the neocortex, where they form vertical stacks of cells, inside-out. These stacks become microcolumns consisting of around 100 neurons. They are isolated by zones with only few cell bodies. Microcolumns have been described as the smallest functional unit of the neocortex. If a sensory area is stimulated with a specific type of input, a spread collection of microcolumns will be active, while areas in between will stay mostly dormant, resulting in a characteristic pattern. However, microcolumns also exhibit a great number of synapses and are therefore heavily linked with their environment. If microcolumns were the basic units of the neocortex, they would have to share a common cellular circuit. However, a circuit of this kind embedded in microcolumns has not been found to date. Some scientists even think that the microcolumn has no modular function at all.

Hypercolumns span larger areas and therefore contain many microcolumns. The exact number of microcolumns varies a lot, from dozens to a few hundred. Hypercolumns are described as a cluster of cells that share receptive field characteristics. For the visual cortex, hypercolumns are defined as a unit containing a full set of values for any given receptive field parameter. They contain all the microcolumns needed to include every one of the aforementioned patterns. In other areas, columns have been found that are responsive to specific objects in sight. However, neighbouring hypercolumns can be overlapping and mostly borderless. They can have very different forms and functions in different areas of the cortex. Some types of hypercolumns are absent in many species at all. These observations are contradictory to the view of hypercolumns as an universal processing unit.

While cortical columns are especially well-documented for the sensory areas, they can also be found in motor and association areas. Columns of cells in the primary motor cortex have been found, that are responsive to a specific direction of movement.

Conclusions

The neocortex displays some features that are common to every area but at the same time, big differences in the cytoarchitecture can be observed. The seven layers are a key feature of the neocortex, however, even those vary between sensory and motor areas. Cortical columns have been described by some scientists as repetitive units for parallel information processing. On the other hand, there is no strong evidence to support the idea of columns being the fundamental organization entity common to all cortex.

These findings come as no surprise if one recalls all the very different tasks the neocortex has to achieve. A fundamental organization entity or functional unit would definitely play into scientists hands. Understanding one unit could then lead to knowledge on way larger parts of the neocortex. However, no structure supporting these requirements has been found to date. If there exists something like a general processing algorithm or circuit on which most of the functionality of the neocortex is based, remains unknown.
